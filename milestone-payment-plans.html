Milestone-based payment plans

Payment structures tied to real case progress for each tax resolution type. Each milestone requires a payment before the case advances — keeping clients engaged, responsive, and financially committed throughout the lifecycle.

Front-loaded to secure commitment — 31% collected on the call. Each subsequent payment unlocks the next phase of IRS engagement. Why this works for the client

They never pay for work that hasn't been done. Each payment unlocks a tangible next step — transcript analysis, financial assessment, IRS submission, and final approval. The heaviest document burden (milestone 2) is gated behind a payment, which creates natural accountability: "We need your bank statements and your second payment to move forward." Clients who go dark after milestone 1 have only cost you $2,500 worth of effort (POA filing and transcripts), not the full case.

What if the client stops paying?

Case pauses at whatever milestone they've reached. All work completed to that point is earned and non-refundable. POA remains active so the IRS contacts your firm, not the client — which creates continued motivation for the client to re-engage. Agreement should specify that payments are earned upon receipt and tied to milestones completed, not outcomes achieved.

Why this works for the client

OICs are the longest and most uncertain resolution path. Spreading payments over 5 milestones across 6–24 months means the client is never more than $2,000–$3,000 away from their next milestone. The heaviest payment is on signing when urgency is highest. By milestone 4, they've already invested $7,500 — psychologically, they're committed to seeing it through.

What if the client stops paying?

The OIC submission (milestone 3) is the critical gate. If a client stalls after milestone 2, you've done the investigation and financial analysis but haven't submitted to the IRS — so your exposure is contained. The client's own 20% lump sum payment to the IRS is due at the same milestone as your payment 3, which creates aligned incentives: they're paying both you and the IRS at the same time to move forward.

CNC clients are by definition in financial hardship — they literally can't pay the IRS. Keeping the payment plan to just 3 milestones with the lowest single payment at $1,500 makes it manageable. The outcome is tangible and fast: "The IRS agreed to leave you alone." That's a powerful motivator to push through the document gathering at milestone 2.

Upsell opportunity

CNC clients whose CSED is 5+ years away are prime candidates for a future OIC once their financial situation stabilises slightly. Annual monitoring check-ins (which can be a separate low-cost retainer) keep the relationship alive for this future conversion.

This is your highest-margin case type and should be positioned as a quick win, often layered on top of another engagement.

Why this works for the client

Penalty abatement has the most immediate, visible ROI of any resolution type. If the client owes $40K and penalties represent $12K of that, a successful abatement just saved them $12K for a $5K fee. That's a 2.4x return they can see on their next IRS statement. The 2-payment structure matches the speed of delivery — often the entire case is resolved within days of signing.

Bundle opportunity

Penalty abatement almost always pairs with another resolution type. Client signs for PA at $5K and IA at $8K = $13K total engagement, structured as a combined 5-payment plan. The PA milestones resolve first (weeks 1–2), then the IA milestones take over (weeks 3–8). This creates early wins that build trust and momentum for the longer IA process.

Why this works for the client

The client is in crisis mode — their money is literally being taken. The first payment buys immediate action (within hours). The second payment is triggered by the thing they care about most: "Your money is unfrozen" or "Your paycheck is whole again." That's a powerful moment to collect because the relief is tangible and emotional. Milestones 3 and 4 transition into the longer-term fix, by which point the client trusts you because you already saved their bank account.

Some clients will want to stop after milestone 2 (levy released) and not continue to the underlying resolution. This is dangerous — without an IA, OIC, or CNC in place, the IRS will simply levy again. The agreement should make clear that milestones 3–4 are not optional add-ons; they're included in the $12K fee specifically because the emergency relief is temporary without them.

Why this works for the client

CDP clients are facing the most aggressive IRS enforcement. The 30-day deadline creates natural urgency that makes the $5K signing payment feel justified — "pay now or lose your rights forever." Once the hearing is filed and collection is suspended, the client has breathing room, and the subsequent payments are spread across months. The milestone structure also makes the $18K total feel manageable: 5 payments averaging $3,600 rather than one intimidating number.

Tax Court escalation

If the CDP determination is unfavorable and the client wants to petition Tax Court (30-day deadline), this is the one scenario where attorney involvement is required — EAs/CPAs cannot represent in Tax Court. The $18K fee covers through the determination. A Tax Court petition should be quoted as a separate engagement, typically $10K–$25K additional depending on complexity. This should be disclosed in the original agreement as a potential additional cost.
